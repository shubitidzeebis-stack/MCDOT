There are two kinds of buyers active in the trucking-LLC market right
now: operators and brokers. They look identical in the first email
and they price LLCs in the same range. The structural difference shows
up at exactly the wrong moment &mdash; usually during diligence,
sometimes at closing.

Knowing which kind of buyer you&rsquo;re dealing with up front is one
of the most useful things a seller can do.

## The broker model

A broker doesn&rsquo;t operate trucks. They source LLCs, repackage
them, and resell to a third-party buyer. Their margin comes from the
spread between what they pay you and what they sell to the next buyer
for. That&rsquo;s a legitimate business, but it has predictable
side-effects:

- **Their offer depends on a buyer they haven&rsquo;t closed yet.**
  Verbal offers from brokers often shrink at closing because their
  end-buyer pushed back.
- **Diligence is shallow.** The broker isn&rsquo;t the one running the
  LLC after closing, so they don&rsquo;t need to verify operational
  details &mdash; the next buyer does, on a different timeline.
- **Closing timelines slip.** Two transactions stacked on each other
  means twice as many bank holds, twice as many lawyers, twice as
  many things that can go sideways.
- **Your identity gets shopped.** Brokers send LLC details to multiple
  potential end-buyers. Even with NDAs, the surface area of who knows
  about your sale grows.

## The operator model

An operator buys an LLC to run it. They have insurance underwriters
on retainer, a bank with established relationships, drivers ready to
onboard, and dispatch capacity to absorb new routes. Their margin
comes from operating the LLC profitably over years &mdash; not from
flipping it.

The downstream effects are the opposite of the broker model:

- **Their offer is firm.** They&rsquo;re committing their own funds,
  not a third party&rsquo;s. Numbers in the LOI match numbers at
  closing.
- **Diligence is thorough.** They actually need to know the LLC&rsquo;s
  operational state because they&rsquo;re about to operate it.
- **Closing timelines are predictable.** One transaction, one bank
  wire, one purchase agreement.
- **Your identity stays narrow.** The LLC information doesn&rsquo;t
  get circulated to potential resale targets, because there aren&rsquo;t
  any.

## How to tell which one you&rsquo;re dealing with

A few questions cut through the noise:

1. **&ldquo;Are you the operator or are you re-selling?&rdquo;** Direct
   and the answer reveals a lot. Operators say yes immediately. Brokers
   often say &ldquo;we work with a network of operators&rdquo; &mdash;
   that&rsquo;s a re-sell.

2. **&ldquo;Where will the LLC operate after closing?&rdquo;** Operators
   can answer with specifics &mdash; lanes, depots, dispatch markets.
   Brokers say &ldquo;wherever the new owner chooses.&rdquo;

3. **&ldquo;Who pays legal fees?&rdquo;** Most operators pay their own
   side. Brokers often try to pass legal costs to the seller.

4. **&ldquo;Can I see the wire confirmation timing?&rdquo;** Operators
   wire same-day or next-day after signing. Brokers often have a
   72-hour or 7-day &ldquo;processing window&rdquo; because they&rsquo;re
   waiting on their end-buyer&rsquo;s wire to clear first.

## Practical takeaway

Selling to an operator is usually faster, more predictable, and
quieter than selling to a broker. The trade-off is that operators have
specific criteria (we don&rsquo;t buy every LLC every broker might
accept, because we have to actually run it). But for sellers who fit,
the experience is meaningfully better.
